Abstract.

We consider electroweak baryogenesis mediated by embedded domain walls. Embedded domain walls originating from a symmetry breaking phase transition are stabilized by thermal plasma effects so that the electroweak symmetry is unbroken in their cores. Then, the cosmological evolution of such domain walls can generate a sufficient baryon asymmetry, irrespective of the order of the electroweak phase transition. For embedded domain walls, the condition not to overdominate the energy of the universe is relaxed significantly and is shown to be compatible with our scenario of electroweak baryogenesis.

I Introduction

The origin of the baryon asymmetry is one of the greatest mysteries of modern cosmology and particle physics. The magnitude of the asymmetry is determined by two methods. One method is Big Bang nucleosynthesis, which yields η≡nB/nγ=3.4-6.9×10-10ηsubscriptnBsubscriptnγ3.46.9superscript1010\eta\equiv n_{{B}}/n_{{\gamma}}=(3.4-6.9)\times 10^{{-10}} (2), where nBsubscriptnBn_{{B}} is the net baryon number density and nγsubscriptnγn_{{\gamma}} is the photon number density. The other is the study of the angular power spectrum of cosmic microwave background anisotropies (changing the baryon number density changes the height of the acoustic peaks in the spectrum), which yields η=6.14±0.25×10-10ηplus-or-minus6.140.25superscript1010\eta=(6.14\pm 0.25)\times 10^{{-10}} (3). These two estimates are consistent within the 95% confidence level.

Electroweak baryogenesis is one of the most attractive scenarios to explain a net baryon asymmetry because very little physics beyond the Standard Model (SM) of particle physics must be assumed. In particular, as first emphasized in (4), by embedding the SM in standard Big Bang cosmology it is possible to satisfy Sakharov’s conditions for successful baryogenesis, that is, (1) having baryon number violating processes present, (2) these processes being C and CP violating, and (3) having deviations from thermal equilibrium (5). In particular, the baryon number violating processes in the SM are generated through the chiral anomaly.

Unfortunately, the minimal SM cannot produce a large enough baryon asymmetry because the amount of CP violation is too small and, in addition, it is difficult to achieve large deviations from thermal equilibrium (see e.g. (6) for recent reviews of electroweak baryogenesis). Thus, to realize electroweak baryogenesis an extension of the SM must be considered. Sufficient CP violation can, for example, be obtained by considering two-Higgs models which contain explicitly CP violating terms in the Higgs mass matrix in addition to the small amount of CP violation in the fermion mass matrix present in the SM.

Nucleation and expansion of critical bubbles — which are produced if the phase transition is strongly first order — are processes out of thermal equilibrium. In the SM the phase transition is not strongly first order. However, a sufficiently strong phase transition can be realized by considering supersymmetric extensions of the standard model. However, in the minimal extension, severe conditions are required: the Higgs bosons must have masses just above the present experimental bounds and the stop must be lighter than the top quark (7).

Another scenario to satisfy the out-of-equilibrium condition is to consider topological defects in the cores of which the electroweak symmetry is unbroken (8). Specifically, in (8) the role of electroweak strings (9) (which are non-topological solitons arising in the standard model and its extensions) was explored. It was shown that the contraction of loops of such string generates a departure from thermal equilibrium, just like the expansion of a bubble wall does. In theories which admit defects, defects are formed independent of the order of the phase transition. Thus, the out-of-equilibrium state is realized even if the electroweak phase transition is of second order. However, electroweak strings are unstable for realistic values of the Weinberg angle (10).

As an extension of the above idea, one can consider cosmic strings originating from a symmetry breaking that takes place before the electroweak phase transition (11). If the electroweak symmetry is restored and sphaleron processes are unsuppressed in the defect cores, then electroweak baryogenesis is possible. Initially (11), this baryogenesis scenario was discussed taking into account only local processes. It was shown that baryogenesis is most efficient when the new symmetry breaking scale is just above the electroweak scale (in this case, the density of strings present at the electroweak symmetry breaking scale is largest). Later, the analysis was improved by including decay and nonlocal effects, which makes the defect-mediated mechanism more efficient (12). Nonetheless, the amplitude of the baryon asymmetry which could be produced this way still lies below the observed value. The main reason for the lack of power of the mechanism is that cosmic strings are one-dimensional objects and therefore, even if the strings are moving relativistically, they sweep out too small a volume of space. An additional constraint (13) is that the defects must be thick enough such that the electroweak sphalerons fit into their cores.

On the other hand, a scaling network (see e.g. (14); (15); (16) for reviews of topological defects in cosmology) of domain walls moving at relativistic speeds will sweep out a large fraction of the universe. Thus, a sufficient baryon asymmetry can be easily generated by making use of domain walls, provided that the microscopic Lagrangian contains enough CP violation (12). However, domain walls produced at energy scales comparable or higher than the electroweak scale quickly dominate the energy density of the universe, which causes severe cosmological problems. Thus, to render domain wall-mediated electroweak baryogenesis viable, we must introduce ad hoc processes that remove domain walls at some time after the electroweak symmetry breaking but before the time of nucleosynthesis. For example, a slight tilt is often introduced into the scalar field potential, which breaks the degeneracy of the vacua such that domain walls decay.

In this paper, we consider another method for wall-mediated electroweak baryogenesis which avoids the domain wall problem. Instead of stable domain walls we make use of embedded domain walls. These have the nice feature that their energy density decreases with the expansion of the universe. Embedded defects (17) of a field theory are solutions of the classical field equations whose energy distribution looks like that of a topological defect. However, in contrast to topological defects, they are not topologically stable and thus decay in the vacuum. However, if some of the fields are fixed, then the field configuration becomes a topological defect in the constrained space.

It was recently argued (18) that many types of embedded defects can be stabilized by interactions with a thermal plasma. To illustrate this effect, let us consider a multi-component real scalar field with a “Mexican hat” type potential. We will consider the epoch after spontaneous symmetry breaking when the scalar field is no longer in thermal equilibrium. In the case that all scalar fields except one are charged, the charged fields acquire thermal masses from gauge interactions with the photon bath (photons are still in thermal equilibrium). If the thermal mass is sufficiently large, then the symmetry is broken only in direction of the neutral scalar field. In this way, embedded domain walls are produced and stabilized. If the square of the induced plasma mass exceeds the absolute value of the negative square mass of the scalar field at the top of its potential, the symmetry is restored in the center of the defect. As the temperature decreases further, the thermal masses also decrease, so that a core phase transition occurs (19). This means that the symmetric state of the charged scalar fields in the core evolves into a slightly asymmetric state, without destroying the overall defect. Thus, the domain wall configurations survive even for small thermal masses. However, the properties and cosmological evolution of these domain walls differ from those of standard ones, as shown later. The conditions necessary to avoid the cosmological domain wall problem are thereby relaxed significantly.

In the next section, we investigate the properties and cosmological evolution of embedded domain walls. Following on, electroweak baryogenesis mediated by these defects is discussed. In the final section, we give our discussion and conclusions.

IV Discussion and Conclusions

In this paper, we discuss electroweak baryogenesis in the context of embedded domain walls. The walls are stabilized by thermal plasma effects so that the electroweak symmetry is restored and sphaleron processes are active inside their cores. Then, as we show, a sufficient baryon asymmetry can easily be produced during the cosmological evolution of the wall network. Different from the case of stable cosmic strings, the volume factor which suppresses defect-mediated baryogenesis from a baryogenesis process which is effectively uniform in space is not significant because domain walls are two-dimensional objects, which, when relativistically moving, sweep out a fraction of order unity of space. This makes it easy to produce a sufficient baryon asymmetry.

In general, a network of stable domain walls will easily dominate the energy density of the universe, leading to a severe cosmological problem. But, in our case, the domain walls are not stable. They are stabilized by thermal plasma and undergo a core phase transition at some critical temperature. This transition reduces their energy per unit area (the tension of the domain walls decreases in proportion to the cosmic temperature), thus leading to a significant relaxation of the condition not to overdominate the energy of the universe. The walls may even decay completely after recombination.

Note that our scenario requires the use of a Coleman-Weinberg potential as opposed to a potential in which the scalar field has a positive square mass at the origin. In spite of this caveat, the positive results of this investigation warrant a further investigation of the fate of embedded defects stabilized by thermal plasmas. This is a topic of future work.
